For a while, I've wanted to build my own personal computer. Over the past year I spent free time lurking on subreddits, watching build videos on YouTube, and reading articles on computer parts. Now that it's the summer, I figured I would finally get around to putting one together!

Reading about PC building is fairly easy, but actually planning and executing a build seemed quite daunting. To make it easier, I kept track ofthe process to help myself plan everything. As with many projects, my pc build has gone through several iterations, changing as I learned more about computers, and also as I rearranged my priorities (more below). Without further ado, here is the documentation for my first PC build.

Usage

The personal computer is different for everyone. The first step was to establish how I would be using the computer. Frankly, I don't game very much. This was an important thing to note, especially because it influences the necessary specifications I would need. Here are some of the ways I expect to use the computer:

  • Video Streaming
  • School Work
  • Programming
  • Potential light gaming

SSD

From the onset, I knew I wanted an SSD in addition to the HDD. It is commonly agreed that the SSD results in the single greatest improvement in computer performance for its price. By storing the operating system on the SSD, I was able to have a boot-up speed of ~10 seconds! Besides the operating system, the SSD is useful for storing files and programs that I often use. The few games I play are saved to the SSD so that they may load faster. Most files that I work with frequently (whatever programs that I'm working on) are also stored on the SSD.

Choosing an SSD actaullly required more research and thought than I had predicted. After doing some reading, I found out that there are two main types of SSDs. In terms of form factor, there are two main standards being m.2 and SATA. The m.2 SSD connects directly to the motherboard, while the SATA SSD is mounted seperately (similar to HDD). The SATA protocol also requires cables to transfer data from drive to motherboard. However, the other popular protocol, NVMe, does not require cables due to its direct connection into the motherboard (through PCI-E). This results in slightly faster data transfer.

After some consideration, I decided to go with the m.2 NVMe drive. Although the difference in speed is only marginal compared to SATA SSDs, there was a good deal for the one I picked up so I didn't mind spending extra. As a first time buider, having fewer cables to manage is also beneficial.
